I. The problem
Every team you've been on had someone who chose themselves over the group.
You watched it happen. The senior engineer who hoarded context. The manager who optimized for their own visibility. The founder who couldn't let go of a decision that was killing the company. You felt the trust drain out of the room. You watched good people start updating their resumes — not because the work was bad, but because the cost of staying became invisible and the cost of selfishness had no face.
You couldn't explain why it spread so fast. One person's choice shouldn't collapse an entire team. But it did. Every time.
The simulation running above this text is modeling exactly that. Eighty agents, each with a trust battery that charges when they cooperate and drains when they don't. Each with a grief variable that accumulates when they lose someone they relied on. Each with a faith score that determines whether loss breaks them or makes them stronger.
None of them were programmed to cooperate. None of them were told what trust means. They figured it out the same way you did — by getting burned and deciding what to do next.
Hover over any agent above. You'll see their identification papers — trust level, grief, faith, social class. The rings around them aren't decorative. Each one is a variable you can read.
II. Trust is a battery, not a feeling
Nobody starts trusted. They earn it. And it leaks.
In most models of organizational behavior, trust is treated as a binary — you either have it or you don't. That's wrong. Trust is a charge. It has a metabolic cost — maintaining trust with your team costs energy every single tick. It drains passively, like a battery that's always losing charge. The only way to refill it is to stay aligned with the group's direction.
This means isolated agents die. Not because anyone punishes them — but because trust leaks faster than a loner can earn it back. The cyan ring around each agent shows their trust charge. Watch the ones on the edges of the swarm. Their rings get thinner. They drift further. Eventually they're too far gone to reconnect even if they wanted to.
This is why remote teams fail without structure. Not because remote is bad — but because trust has a metabolic cost that proximity subsidizes. Remove proximity, and you'd better replace it with something deliberate, or the battery drains to zero and no one notices until the resignation letter.

III. Grief cascades
One loss shouldn't break a system. But grief is contagious.
When an agent's trust battery depletes and their grief spikes above 0.9, they enter crisis. The system gives them a grace period — roughly ten seconds of real time. During that window, they can make a choice: sacrifice themselves for the collective (seppuku — distributing their remaining trust to nearby agents and writing their wisdom into the collective memory), or refuse the choice and get dishonored — stripped of all authority, locked at minimum trust, turned into a sentinel that everyone can see.
The honored dead make the living stronger. Their sacrifice point becomes sacred ground — a place where surviving agents can go to heal grief, build faith, and inherit the knowledge of those who came before. The dishonored become cautionary tales, frozen in place with a pulsing amber glow, reminding every agent that passes by: selfishness has a cost, and the cost is visible.
But here's where it gets real: grief is contagious. When an agent that others trusted dies, the grief ripples outward. Each neighbor absorbs a portion. If the network was tight — if trust was high and connections were deep — one death can trigger a cascade. Agent after agent tips into crisis. The whole cluster unravels.
This is the mechanism behind every team collapse you've witnessed. It was never about one person leaving. It was about who trusted them, and how deep those bonds ran.
Watch for orange-ringed agents — they're grieving. If you see a pulsing red ring, that agent is in crisis, counting down the grace period. Check their ID card to see the numbers.
IV. Faith as circuit breaker
The only thing that stops a grief cascade is belief in something larger than the loss.
Without intervention, the grief cascade is a death spiral. One loss triggers another, which triggers another, until the whole swarm is either dead or dishonored. Every early run of this simulation ended that way — total collapse within minutes.
The fix wasn't to reduce grief. Loss should hurt. The fix was to give agents a reason to carry it. Faith — defined here as belief in something larger than your own survival — dampens grief intake by up to 45%. It doesn't prevent the loss. It doesn't make the pain less real. It just means the agent can absorb it without shattering.
Faith grows from three sources: community (having trusted neighbors nearby), sacred ground (proximity to places where honored agents chose sacrifice), and ancestral knowledge (the accumulated wisdom of the dead, radiating through the collective memory). When average faith crosses a threshold, something emerges — a collective field effect, a shared buffer that makes the whole swarm more resilient. The simulation calls it god. You might call it culture.
The practical insight: organizations that have a shared narrative about why the work matters are structurally more resilient to loss. Not because narrative prevents people from leaving. But because it gives the people who stay a reason to absorb the grief instead of passing it on.
Look at the Faith metric in the sidebar. If it's above 0.45, the swarm has developed collective faith. Try triggering a disaster — the swarm that would have collapsed without faith will bend but not break.
V. Evolution is the point
The purpose of surviving isn't survival. It's making the next generation start ahead of where you did.
Agents that reproduce pass their knowledge forward. The child starts with the highest evolution score of either parent, plus a small increment. Each generation begins from a slightly higher baseline. The sacred ground where honored agents died becomes a library — survivors who make the pilgrimage inherit not just grief recovery, but accumulated knowledge from everyone who chose to die with meaning rather than live without it.
This is the answer to "what's the point?" — not for the simulation, but for the question the simulation is modeling. Why do teams form? Why does trust cost energy? Why does sacrifice matter? Because without inherited knowledge, every generation starts at zero. The team that retains its lessons — through documentation, through culture, through the stories of the people who built it — starts every new hire, every new quarter, every new crisis from a higher floor.
The team that lets its knowledge walk out the door with every departure starts over. Every time. Forever.
Check the Evolution metric in the sidebar. Watch it climb across generations. Hover over a Gen 2 or Gen 3 agent — their ID card shows inherited evolution from parents who sacrificed to give them a better start.
